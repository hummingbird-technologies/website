Inspired by Kiva Systems, a former mobile robotics startup now operating as Amazon Robotics, Hummingbird Technologies uses an innovative design to create their own automated multifunctional logistics robot with the potential for further optimizing operations at inventory distribution (four corner-mounted, independently steered and powered wheel modules) allow for fully decoupled translation and continuous rotation of the drive base at over 1 meters pers second, all while carrying a weight of 500 pounds!

As such, it is more maneuverable and cost-effective than its predecessor, ready to revolutionize the warehouse logistics robot.



Mechanical Design


To achieve fully-independent translation and rotation along with continuous rotation, existing swerve drivetrains often have two major drawbacks. Their innate mechanical complexity often leads to elevated part counts, and emphasis on packing factor and minimizing weight rarely allow design for manufacturability (DFM) or design for assembly (DFA) considerations to prevail. Because of the perceived industrial applicationâ€™s emphasis on durability and maintenance, the design sought to achieve the desired 500 lb load rating while minimizing part count and achieving better DFM results.

Replacing spur geartrains with timing belt reductions on the drive stage and an off-the-shelf planetary gearbox (linked to the wheel module by a 1:1 belt) on the turning stage reduced the amount of hardware and number of custom parts.

The wheel module itself also minimizes part count and eases manufacturing complexity by utilizing a single-piece aluminum extrusion sleeve with externally captured bearings.

Electrical Design


With 4 modules each with two motors, one for rotation and one for translation, the robot packs a lot of electrical complexity. Each motor is driven by a motor controller that receives PWN input and has an encoder for measuring its position. Inputs and outputs from each of the eight motors make their way to the myRIO micro-controller via a set of bread boards.

For meeting our target load of 500 pounds, we use CIM motors for translation which can draw upto 60 A at 12 V! We outfitted the robot with a 150 A breaker and individual fuses on each motor power line. We have a big red "E-stop" button on the breaker. And we use two wheelchair batteries to power the whole setup!

Since the electric circuit shared by the motor and motor controllers can often have very strange transients during operation, we use a separate 11.1 V LiPo battery to power the myRIO and router bundle.

Software Design


The myRIO is programmed in LabView. The code was architected with a number of abstraction levels. At the lowest level, we programmed the myRIO's FPGA to interpret readings from the absolute encoders we use to measure rotation.

We then combine encoder input and motor controller PWM output modules into PID controllers. Rotation and Velocity PID Control is put together to get Module control ie. control of one of the four modules. These are then put together to get Platform Control at the highest level.

In addition, we tuned the control parameters of each module separately since the control characteristics of each motor and motor controller, and even the mechanical parameters of every module component were slightly different for the four modules.

Graphical User Interface


The robot is controlled with a gamepad or a joystick wirelessly. It therefore needs no digital GUI for feeding in inputs.

However, we created an interface for outputing the speed and pointing direction of each module of the robot. This interface is meant for analyzing the performance of the robot. An operator can look at these outputs and potentially change the control parameters of the robot to achieve better performance.
